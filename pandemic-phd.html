PhD during a Pandemic

I started my PhD programme on January 1, 2020 - the very first thing I did in the very first moments of a new decade. Two months into the PhD, the pandemic broke out and in about 75 days spending life back in academics, I was sent home packing. It is now August, the supposed peak of the pandemic and 5 months into working towards my PhD from home.

Firstly, I would like to express gratitude to my advisor, my department and institute for functioning positively like nothing happened - raising the expectations, keeping up with work, hosting classes and meetings online and most importantly, working towards beating the pandemic in the many ways we can; at the very least by staying at home to protect the health services. I would also thank PMRF for extending support during these tough times. It has certainly made work easier.

The first two years of my PhD work are primarily dedicated towards coursework. I have had one semester completed, and I took two courses – Time-Frequency Analysis and Convex Optimisation. These are relevant to the focus of my research in sampling theory and signals with finite rate of innovation. In many cases, it is possible to model signal reconstruction as an inverse problem where the signal parameters can be solved for using robust solvers. Such ideas have proved best in the area of compressive sensing, where the signal can be reconstructed using few measurements by using the underlying signal structure - being sparse in some known domain. In particular, it is popular in MR image reconstruction since the acquisition time is significantly reduced. Finite-Rate-of-Innovation theory is an analogue model for sparse signals. Within this framework, signals are sampled at rates much lower than its Nyquist rate. In theory, this can handle sampling and reconstruction of sparse nonbandlimited signals.

Recently, my area of focus has been on time-based sampling of signals. This is an interesting area within sampling theory as the samplers proposed are energy efficient. The samplers or ‘time-encoding machines’ are devices that represent continuous-time signals with discrete temporal values - and the question at hand is if this representation is sufficient to reconstruct the continuous-time signal. We investigate the time-based sampling of continuous-time finite-rate-of-innovation signals, where we demonstrate perfect reconstruction is indeed possible from the temporal information recorded using different time-encoding machines. This work was published at the International Conference on Acoustics, Speech and Signal Processing, (ICASSP) 2020, Barcelona that took place virtually. Further, we have explored such sampling schemes in multiple channels and in the presence of noise. Such sampling schemes are of interest due to the increasing popularity of neuromorphic cameras. These are event-based cameras that record videos at very high frame-rates and at very high dynamic range.

Lastly, for the readers, I wish you good health and a great time indoors. It has been a tough few months and I hope everything goes back to normal as soon as one can imagine.
